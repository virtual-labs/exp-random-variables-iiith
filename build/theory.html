Set theory :

A set is a collection of objects, either concrete or abstract. In the study of probability we are particularly interested in the set of all outcomes of a random experiment and subsets of their set.

Definition 1 (Union of sets) :

The Union of two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F is the set of all elements that are in atleast one of the sets EEE or FFF.

Definition 2 (Intersection of sets) :

The Intersection of two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F is the set of all elements that are common to both sets EEE and FFF.

Definition 3 (Compliment of a set) :

The Compliment of a set EEE is the set of all elements that are not in EEE.

Definition 4 (Difference of two sets) :

The difference of two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F is the set of all elements that are in EEE but not in FFF. It is denoted by E−FE - FE−F or E\FE \backslash FE\F.

Definition 5 (Exclusive-or of two sets) :

The Exclusive-or of two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F is the set of all elements that are either in EEE or in FFF but not in both. It is defined as E⊕F=(E−F)∪(F−E) \begin{align*} E \oplus F = (E-F) \cup (F-E) \end{align*} E⊕F=(E−F)∪(F−E)​

Definition 6 (DeMorgan's laws) :

For any two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F,

  • (E∪F)c=Ec∩Fc(E \cup F)^{c} = E^{c} \cap F^{c}(E∪F)c=Ec∩Fc

  • (E∩F)c=Ec∪Fc(E \cap F)^{c} = E^{c} \cup F^{c}(E∩F)c=Ec∪Fc

Definition 7 (Partition of a set) :

Given any set EEE, an n−n-n−partition of a EEE consists of a sequence of sets EiE_iEi​, iii = 1, 2, 3, ⋯\cdots⋯, n such that Ei⊂E,  ⋃i=1nEi=E,  &  Ei∩Ej=ϕ,  ∀i≠j \begin{align*} E_i \subset E , \; \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} E_i = E, \; \& \; E_i \cap E_j = \phi, \; \forall i \neq j \end{align*} Ei​⊂E,i=1⋃n​Ei​=E,&Ei​∩Ej​=ϕ,∀i=j​

Definition 8 (Equality of sets) :

Two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F are said to be equal if every element of in EEE is in FFF and vice versa. E=F if E⊂F  &  F⊂E \begin{align*} E = F \text{ if } E \subset F \; \& \; F \subset E \end{align*} E=F if E⊂F&F⊂E​

Definition 9 (Disjoint sets) :

Two sets E  &  FE \; \& \; FE&F are said to be disjoint if E∩F=ϕE \cap F = \phiE∩F=ϕ.

Probability :

A mathematical theory of probability will incorporate those concepts of chance which are expressed and implicit in common rational understanding. Let AAA be an event, and the chance of AAA occuring is ppp. The occurrence or non occurence of AAA depends upon a chain of circumstances involved. This chain is called an experiment of trial. The result of the experiment is called its outcome.

Any experiment involving randomness can be modelled as a probability space. The space comprises of

  • Ω\OmegaΩ : Set of possible outcomes of the experiment
  • F\mathcal{F}F : Set of events
  • P\mathbb{P}P : Probability measure

Definition 1 (Sample space) :

The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment of an experiment is called the sample space. Denoted by Ω\OmegaΩ.

Example 1 : In the scenario of a coin being tossed, Ω={H,T}\Omega = \{ H, T\}Ω={H,T}

Example 2 : In the scenario of a dice being rolled, Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}\Omega = \{ 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}

An event can be defined as a subset of the appropriate sample space Ω\OmegaΩ. If Ω={H,T}\Omega = \{ H, T\}Ω={H,T}, then an event AAA cab be {H}\{H\}{H} or {H}c\{H\}^{c}{H}c or {H}∩{T}\{H\} \cap \{T\}{H}∩{T} or else if Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}\Omega = \{ 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 \}Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}, then AAA can be {2,4,6}\{2, 4, 6\}{2,4,6} or {1,2,3}\{1, 2, 3\}{1,2,3} or {2}c\{2\}^{c}{2}c. The compliment of a subset AAA of Ω\OmegaΩ is denoted as AcA^{c}Ac. Whenever A  &  BA \; \& \; BA&B are events in which we are interested to pursure, then we can reasonable concern ourselves with the events A∩BA \cap BA∩B, A∪BA \cup BA∪B, amd AcA^{c}Ac respectively. Two events A  &  BA \; \& \; BA&B are disjoint if their intersection is the empty set ϕ\phiϕ.

  • ϕ\phiϕ is said to be the impossible event
  • Ω\OmegaΩ is said to be the certain event since some member of Ω\OmegaΩ will ceetainly occur.

All the subsets of Ω\OmegaΩ need not be events.

  3. If A1,A2,⋯A_1, A_2, \cdotsA1​,A2​,⋯ is a collection of disjoint members of F\mathcal{F}F, in that Ai∩Aj=ϕA_i \cap A_j = \phiAi​∩Aj​=ϕ for all pairs i,ji, ji,j satisfying i≠ji \neq ji=j then

This triple (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})(Ω,F,P) is called as a probability space. Conditional probability :

An experiment is repeated NNN times, and on each occasion we observe the occurrences (or non occurrences) of two events AAA and BBB. We observe that the probabilty that AAA occurs given BBB occurs should be reasonably defined as P(A∩B)P(B)\begin{align*} \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)} \end{align*} P(B)P(A∩B)​​

Probabilistic intuition also leads to the same conclusion. Given that event BBB occurs, if it is the case that event AAA occurs if and only P(A∩B)\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)P(A∩B) occurs. The conditional probability of AAA given BBB should be proportional to P(A∩B)\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)P(A∩B).

  • P(A∣B)=αP(A∩B)\mathbb{P}(A|B) = \alpha \mathbb{P} (A \cap B) P(A∣B)=αP(A∩B) for some const α\alphaα
  • The conditional property of Ω\OmegaΩ given BBB must be equal to 111
  • Thus αP(Ω∩B)=1\alpha \mathbb{P} (\Omega \cap B) = 1αP(Ω∩B)=1
  • ⇒αP(B)=1\Rightarrow \alpha \mathbb{P} (B) = 1⇒αP(B)=1
  • ⇒α=1P(B)\Rightarrow \alpha = \frac{1}{\mathbb{P} (B)} ⇒α=P(B)1​

Definition 4 (Conditional Probability) :

If P(B)>0\mathbb{P}(B) > 0P(B)>0, then the conditional probability that AAA occurs given that BBB occurs is defined as P(A∣B)=P(A∩B)P(B) \begin{align*} \mathbb{P}(A|B) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)} \end{align*} P(A∣B)=P(B)P(A∩B)​​

Independence

In general, the occurence of some event BBB changes the probability that another event AAA occurs. Where the original probability P(A)\mathbb{P}(A)P(A) being replaced by P(A∣B)\mathbb{P}(A|B)P(A∣B). If the original probability remains unchanged, then we say that the two events A  &  BA \; \& \; BA&B are independent.

P(A∣B)=P(A∣B) \begin{align*} \mathbb{P}(A|B) =\mathbb{P}(A|B) \end{align*} P(A∣B)=P(A∣B)​

Events A  &  BA \; \& \; BA&B are called independent events if P(A∩B)=P(A)∗P(B)\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = \mathbb{P}(A) * \mathbb{P}(B)P(A∩B)=P(A)∗P(B). If the family of events {Ai:i∈I}\{A_i : i \in I\}{Ai​:i∈I} has the property that P(Ai∩Aj)=P(Ai)∗P(Aj)∀i≠j\mathbb{P}( A_i\cap A_j) = \mathbb{P}(A_i) * \mathbb{P}(A_j) \forall i \neq jP(Ai​∩Aj​)=P(Ai​)∗P(Aj​)∀i=j then it is called pairwaise independent set of events. P(A∩B∣C)=P(A∣C)∗P(B∣C) \begin{align*} \mathbb{P}(A \cap B|C) =\mathbb{P}(A|C) * \mathbb{P}(B|C) \end{align*} P(A∩B∣C)=P(A∣C)∗P(B∣C)​

Completeness

Let (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})(Ω,F,P) be a probability space. Any event AAA which has zero probability, that is P(A)=0\mathbb{P}(A) = 0P(A)=0, is called a null event. It may seem reasonable that any subset B⊂AB \subset AB⊂A will itself be null, but this may be without meaning since BBB may not be an event, this P(B)\mathbb{P}(B)P(B) may not be defined.

Definition (Complete Space) :

A probability space (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})(Ω,F,P) is called a complete space if all subsets of null sets are events.

Random Variables

Quantities governed by randomness correspond to functions on the probability space called Random variables. The values taken by a random variable is subject to chance, and the associated likelihoods are described by a function called the distributed function.
